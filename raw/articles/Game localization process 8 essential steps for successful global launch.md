## What is game localization?

Simply put, game localization means adapting a game to a certain market outside the one it was originally released.

The major part of the game localization process is linguistic translation, but localization isn’t limited to language. It also includes culturalization, which means adapting the context and environment of the game so that it better fits the preferences of each target audience.

**It’s important to treat the localization of video games holistically**, not as a process of translating everything from one language to another but as a process of making it relevant to the target region. This might include UI redesigns, character tweaks, or some other significant changes.

With that said, what’s included in game localization?

- **Interface**. [Game UI localization](https://www.gridly.com/blog/game-ui-design-localization-best-practices/) in gaming will adapt the experience for each region and audience. For instance, it makes sense to rethink the position of menu items for left-to-right languages.
- **In-game content**. This is the biggest part of the localization work, which covers all the text that appears in the game: descriptions, inventories, tutorials, subtitles, etc.
- **Dialogue**. For narrative games that feature a lot of characters, dialogue translation will in many ways define the quality of localization.
- **Cultural elements**. This is a more subtle area of localization that sometimes requires you to rethink some events or objects depicted in the game. Different cultures have different attitudes toward symbols, depictions of historical events, topics related to religion, etc. The point of localization (or, in this context, culturalization) is to make sure that there are no offensive elements and that the game is culturally relevant to different locales.
- **Game title**. You can decide whether you want your game title to be translated or not. For instance, if the title is very short or features the protagonist’s name, it’s better to localize it to something more descriptive for the release on East Asian markets.
- **Promotional materials**. Even when the game itself is fully translated, the localization process isn’t over. It’s always a good idea to create ads and marketing campaigns for each market individually, collaborating with local stars and addressing the preferences of a particular audience.

While the types of content you need to translate a game will vary depending on the genre, style, and unique features, there are some universal rules that will help you manage your localization process.

Before we dive deeper into the game localization process, let’s get a quick overview of the benefits game localization can bring.

## Why do you need to localize your game?

Localization is a way for your game to reach a wider demographic and, essentially, increase your revenue. Here’s what you can gain:

- **Access to more markets and players.** With the power of localization, you can reach millions of gamers all over the world and find loyal players who enjoy the type of product you’re creating.
- **Enhanced user experience across the globe.** A lot of non-native speakers play games in English, but the experience of playing in their native language will be much richer. This way, players can feel more connected to the story and everything that is happening in the game.
- **More streams of revenue.** Entering new markets and getting more satisfied players means increasing your revenue. This is particularly important for [game-as-a-service (Gaas)](https://www.gridly.com/blog/games-as-a-service/) models where ongoing player engagement drives long-term success.
- **Understanding of different cultures (for further engagements).** The process of game translation is a great way to learn about other countries and gaming communities there. The insights you learn will help you launch updates or new games with less friction and adjust your games to the preferences of target players.
- **Strengthened brand image in the industry.** Releases in different countries will create an additional buzz about your game, while reviews and comments generated by players will make your brand more recognizable on a global scale.

## 3 major challenges of the game localization process (and how to overcome them)

Before we move to practical guidelines for preparing your game for translation and culturalization, let’s take a look at some of the biggest challenges that game companies face. You can gracefully solve all of them if you prepare your game for localization, build a comprehensive localization team, and use the right game localization tool.

### Challenge 1. Localization as an afterthought

Sometimes, game localization is approached as a process done completely after design and development. With this attitude, companies are risking wasting more time and money on localization because of not being properly prepared for it.

**The solution here is** to consider localization as soon as possible, ideally, at the start of development. You’ll need to understand what languages you’re going to translate the game to place these plans on a general timeline.

When you think about localization beforehand:

- You’ll have game strings and all related materials correctly prepared for the translation
- You’ll have time and flexibility to adjust the designs if needed (for instance, to tweak the UI for right-to-left languages or to replace violent imagery for compliance on strictly regulated markets)

### Challenge 2. Fragmented data

Complex localization projects are prone to data fragmentation issues when a lot of different tools and manual actions are involved in the process. Say, if you’re creating multiple spreadsheets and sharing them with translators, then inputting finished translations into other files for developers to check them, you might face translation errors, data loss, or other issues. It’s just impractical.

**The solution here is** to find the right game localization software that will unify all the information for you and connect all the parties involved in the process.

### Challenge 3. Lack of LQA

Just like you need to think about localization from the earliest stages possible, you also need to have a continuous cycle of testing. Plus, localization QA should cover both linguistic aspects of translation and broader things like cultural sensitivities.

**The solution here is** to find a convenient translation management system that includes LQA capabilities and test your game localizations during development.

## The 8-step game localization process

Let’s explore the steps you should follow to maintain flexible processes and achieve desired outcomes in game localization.

### Step 1. Analyze target markets and prepare a launch plan

The research comes first: knowing what markets you want your game to be published in and when, you’ll need to understand these markets. Here are some of the things to consider:

- **It can be a single country or a whole region that speaks the same language (but with important differences).**

In this regard, you have to decide if you’re making separate versions of the game for each locale or rather creating a version that would encompass several locales.

Generally speaking, it’s always better to localize for each country or region separately to appreciate the local context, sensitivities, regulations, and linguistic nuances. For instance, game publishers sometimes go for a so-called neutral Spanish when localizing their products to Latin America. It’s a cheaper and simpler option in production, but the fact is, there’s no such thing as neutral Spanish: it’s an artificial construct that leaves out local slang and other things that liven up translation.

- **You can organize a simultaneous launch on multiple markets or release the game in the original language first and then proceed with the localization country by country.**

Simultaneous launches are generally recommended for large companies and games franchises that are already globally known. For smaller game studios, it doesn’t make sense to account for the increased costs and more complicated logistics of simultaneous releases. Instead, you’ll have more time to focus on each locale.

- **Some countries might have harsh regulations for game publication, which will require you to plan a longer timeline and potentially change or remove some parts of your game.**

Countries like China or Saudi Arabia have strict rules that apply to topics covered in games and depiction of potentially triggering things. Even if your game doesn’t feature something explicitly prohibited (references to religious symbols, excessive violence, etc.), there might be something that can be considered offensive or not suitable for the target region.

To avoid prolonged reviews and unnecessary rounds of compliance checks, partner with a local company that will help you navigate through the regulations and understand if you need to change something in the game’s content.

- **Note down the major things you’ve learned about target locales and prepare the localization plan.**

Once you’ve done your research and have a list of target markets and desired timelines for each release, you can craft a launch plan that will help you monitor the process. A game localization plan can include all locales and languages you’re planning to have translations in, desired deadlines, allocated budgets, all specialists you need to hire for each locale, and all legal documents you might need to prepare.

### Step 2. Choose a suitable localization management tool

A proper translation management system will help you overview the progress, automate routine tasks, and facilitate collaboration between people involved in development, translation, and QA.

In a [2022 survey](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/362388744_Video_Game_localisation_tools_a_user_survey) about game localization tools, the following features were named most often as essential:

- **Dialogues seen in the right order.** It’s essential for translators not to get confused when dealing with game dialogue. It should always be clear who is the speaker of each particular line of dialogue because the speaker defines the tone of voice, gendered grammar (in languages that feature it), appropriate slang, and so on.
- **Terminology management.** Your game will definitely have a lot of repetitive strings and some terms that are common for the genre or specific to the unique in-game universe. A glossary can automatically pre-fill repeated strings and help translators save time and eliminate errors. Plus, there might be ambiguities with words and concepts that have multiple meanings. In this regard, terminology management in a localization tool will allow you to provide helpful context for each term usage.

Apart from the mentioned features of a game localization platform, we recommend paying attention to the following features:

- **Possibility to set character limitations.** Drastic differences between the same phrases in English and other languages are among the major concerns in the translation process. Having a game localization tool that automatically detects translations that surpass the length limit will save you from additional rounds of reviews and adjustments.

### Step 3. Break down and extract the content for translation

To prepare your game for localization, you’ll need to extract and categorize all the different types of content that exist in your game. This process encompasses the following:

- **Content audit to identify all assets that need to be localized.** Apart from the obvious textual content, see if your game has visual elements that might need translations or cultural adjustments. On top of that, identify which content that supports the game you want to be translated (legal texts, marketing campaigns, etc.).
- **Import to the localization management tool.** You’ll need a robust tool that supports file types convenient for you to import game content to it.
- **Separating platform-specific localizations.** Since many things will differ based on a platform, you should create separate projects for each.
- **Categorization and prioritization of assets.** In your game localization platform, you can create separate projects for different types of content (for example, one for in-game text and another one for voice-over translation) and create different views based on certain parameters (for example, you can mark strings with interface items, dialogues, in-game instructions, etc. differently to quickly distinguish them).

### Step 4. Create a localization kit

A localization kit is a comprehensive document that includes all the essential information needed for translators and other specialists to understand the context of your game, its style and tone, its characters, and its target players.

What do you need to include in your game localization kit?

- **Target audience.** Describe who your game is meant for. Does it have age restrictions? Do players that you’re targeting have specific interests? And so on.
- **Platforms the game is going to be released on.** There will be platform-specific terms related to navigation and other aspects of playing, so you should indicate what platforms and operating systems (in the case of mobile games) you need.
- **General description of the game.** Add a short description to introduce everyone to your game and understand its aesthetics and major functionality.
- **Characters of the game.** Describe each of your game characters and provide visuals. Explain the relationships between characters so that translators understand how to approach the tone of game dialogue (formal, friendly, hostile, etc.). Indicate if it makes sense to localize character’s names and if they have a dialect or other linguistic peculiarities.

- **Any contextual notes that make sense for your game.** The more context you provide, the better for game translation. For instance, if the fictional world inside your game features a lot of specific locations, items, and symbols, you should explain all of these concepts and illustrate them with screenshots.
- **Localization glossary.** A glossary, or a termbase, is a collection of key terms used in a game that is filled out in the original and target languages. It can save time and reduce errors in localization projects as translators and reviewers will be able to quickly find references to terms they don’t know or are not sure about. A game localization glossary can include names of platform-specific interface components, game strings that don’t require translation, industry jargon, abbreviations, etc.

### Step 5. Build a localization team

Whether you collaborate with a localization agency or hire localization specialists internally, you need to understand what roles do you need, how to distribute responsibilities, and how to manage communication between developers and designers on one side and translators and reviews on the other.

Having the right professionals in place can make or break your game localization process. Typically, people involved in video game localization are:

- Translators
- Editors / reviewers
- Localization testers
- Project manager

We’d like to highlight some of the best industry practices in the realm of building and managing a game localization team:

- **Hire translators who like games.** People who are familiar with the gaming culture, especially in your genre, will more quickly and easily understand the context for translation.
- **Give translators and reviewers the opportunity to play and explore your game.** Allocate a certain number of hours for the localization team to get familiarized with your game. This way, they’ll likely eliminate mistakes with ambiguous words, dialogue attribution, etc.
- **Provide tools for communication between development and localization teams.** The game localization software you’re using should make it effortless and easy for all team members to communicate with each other in case translators want to clarify something from developers or some other issues arise.
- **Adjust your game localization tool to your particular team structure and project needs.** It’s crucial to have a platform that will allow you to set different roles, role-based permissions, and different types of views for tasks. ### Step 6. Consider content adjustments to culturalize your game

As we’ve mentioned before, translating a game from one language to another is only a part of localization. To make your game compliant with local regulations and culturally relevant to players in different regions, you need to research and respect some rules and sensitivities of those regions.

What aspects of your game should you analyze to know if you need to adjust something?

- **Depiction of violence and gore.** For releasing your game in some locales (for instance, in Japan, China, and Middle Eastern countries), you’ll need to tone down violent imagery to a certain extent. Sometimes, it requires significant design effort as you’ll need to, say, replace any bone depiction with something else (bones replaced by bread in the Chinese version of World of Warcraft have become a running joke in the gaming community). World of Warcraft and Genshin Impact serve as excellent [video game localization examples](https://www.gridly.com/blog/world-of-warcraft-vs-genshin-impact-good-and-bad-examples-of-video-game-localization/) cases, showcasing both effective and problematic localization approaches across different markets.
- **Nudity and sexual scenes.** You might need to tone down sexual references or redesign nude characters for certain regions. For instance, Cyberpunk 2077 removed the option to customize genitals in the Japanese version, while nudity was covered up by pieces of clothing in The Witcher 3 for the Middle East.
- **Historical events.** If your game reference s some historical figures or real-life events, you’ll need to double-check how they are perceived in the target region.
- **Religious symbols and references.** You won’t have any problems with game releases in secular societies, but for predominantly religious countries, you’ll probably need to remove anything that can be considered blasphemy.

### Step 7. Set rules and prepare for localization testing

Quality assurance is a crucial phase of the game localization process that ensures accuracy and cultural fit. Here’s what you can do to ensure consistent game translation quality testing:

- **Unify QA with localization management.** Making LQA part of your general localization pipeline is one of the key principles of effective LQA. It means that testers report issues in the same environment where translators and other people involved do their jobs, and it ensures a greater level of consistency and makes it easier to monitor all processes.
- **Provide as much contextual and terminology information as possible.** Testers should be able to quickly understand the context: what’s going on in the interface or in the narrative scene, who is speaking to whom in game dialogues, and so on.

Screenshots can be a real lifesaver here. For instance, thanks to the possibility to instantly check screenshots attached next to original and translated strings in Gridly, game developer Rovio 4x their LQA speed.

### Step 8. Implement localization-friendly development practices

These best practices will help you optimize your game localization process from the start. To make your game more localization-friendly from the start, consider implementing these practices:

- **Use Unicode:** Ensure your game engine and all text-handling systems support Unicode (UTF-8 or UTF-16). This is crucial for supporting a wide range of languages, especially those with non-Latin scripts.
- **Design for text expansion:** Different languages require different amounts of space to convey the same message. Design UI elements with flexibility to accommodate text of varying lengths, planning for up to 35% expansion in most cases.
- **Externalize strings:** Store all text in external resource files (e.g., JSON, XML) and use unique string identifiers for each piece of text. This separation of content from code is crucial for efficient localization. For Unreal Engine projects, proper namespace organization is essential for scalable localization - learn more in our [Unreal Engine localization namespace](https://www.gridly.com/blog/unreal-engine-localization-namespaces-how-structure-game-scalability/) guide.
- **Implement careful string interpolation:** When inserting variables into strings, use a system that allows for reordering of elements. Consider using numbered or named placeholders instead of position-based formatting.
- **Handle plurals and grammatical cases:** Implement a system to manage complex language rules for plurals and grammatical cases. Consider using localization libraries that handle these issues.
- **Plan for right-to-left (RTL) languages:** If targeting markets with RTL languages like Arabic or Hebrew, implement a system to mirror UI layouts and handle bidirectional text.
- **Localize non-text assets:** Remember that localization isn’t just about text. Design your asset pipeline to handle localized versions of images, audio, and video.
- **Implement flexible date, time, and number formatting:** Use libraries or built-in functions for locale-aware formatting of dates, times, and numbers.
- **Establish proper version control:** Implementing effective [game development version control](https://www.gridly.com/blog/version-control-in-game-development/) practices ensures that localization assets stay synchronized with your main development branch and prevents conflicts during the translation process.

By implementing these practices from the start of your development process, you’ll create a solid foundation for efficient game localization. This proactive approach can save significant time and resources during the localization phase.
