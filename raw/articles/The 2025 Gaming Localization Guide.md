## What is gaming localization?

Gaming localization translates and adapts a video or mobile game’s language so it feels native to players in different regions.

This goes beyond swapping words.

It’s about making your game’s content and experience **feel culturally relevant and familiar.**

For example, changing voice lines to match regional accents or adjusting in-game currency to reflect local pricing.

While great [content localization](https://xtm.cloud/blog/content-localization/) can make your game a hit in new target markets, doing it poorly can lead to confusion or offense.

Don’t let your epic boss fight become an accidental meme. This often also involves complying with local laws and restrictions.

Take the online multiplayer game *Fortnite*.

In the Chinese localized version, game developers replaced any skeletons or bones to get regulatory approval faster:

Source: [YouTube](https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/watch?v=tZvkJS1wpO8)

Avoiding cultural sensitivities and superstitions around skeletons likely saved serious go-to-market time.

The answer to ‘What is localization in video games?’ wouldn’t be complete without looking at how it shows up in gaming products.

This in-depth form of translation includes nuanced factors like:

- **Following censorship laws**,e.g., removing blood or violent imagery to pass regional content filters
- **Avoiding controversial or banned symbols**, especially in countries with strict historical or political sensitivities
- **Swapping voiceovers and dubbing** — because British and Australian are not the same vibe
- **Adapting currencies and pricing formats** to match local economic expectations and prevent sticker shock
- **Tweaking characters or storylines** so they resonate with players’ values and norms

Planning for game localization from the start helps devs build a dynamic [user interface (UI)](https://xtm.cloud/blog/ui-localization/) that supports multiple languages.

Plus, set up all the behind-the-scenes complexity that makes it feel seamless.

Localization can grow your player base and increase sales, all while improving your studio’s reputation.

If you want people to love your game worldwide, now’s the time to start implementing it.

### Gain wider reach

Localization makes your game playable by people who otherwise wouldn’t give it a shot. That’s potentially millions of new players.

For example, the team behind *Slay the Princess* surveyed its target audience before launch and found that [63% of non-English speakers](https://newsletter.gamediscover.co/p/how-localization-affects-game-discovery) wouldn’t buy the game if it were only in English.

That’s not one missed opportunity. That’s whole markets walking away.

And it’s not just about players understanding your dialogue.

As Simon Carless, founder of The GameDiscoverCo, explains:

> “Your game’s discoverability is affected by having specific languages (Chinese, etc.) selected…from the first day your game’s page is available.”

You could be buried in the algorithm if your store page doesn’t support certain languages from day one. Especially in high-demand regions like China, Korea, and Brazil.

Big studios creating AAA games understand that global hype requires global access.

For example, Rockstar’s job listings for *GTA 6* included multiple roles for localization quality assurance (QA):

Localization makes your game playable and enjoyable for people who don’t speak the original language.

Because let’s face it, no one enjoys guessing what a menu option does or missing story beats because they can’t understand dialogue.

### Build your brand reputation

Localization shows that you care about global players enough to meet them with culturally respectful content in their native language.

Gamers notice that effort. So do streamers, reviewers, and international media.

And that trust can go a long way in the video game industry.

Big studios like Ubisoft invest heavily in entire localization departments for this exact reason:

> “We collaborate as early as possible with our development teams to resolve issues quickly and anticipate localization challenges. We provide our players with the greatest possible gaming experiences, whatever system they’re using and wherever they are.”

Releasing titles in dozens of languages simultaneously builds global fandoms that stick around for the long haul.

But it’s also about staying out of legal hot water.

Some governments, like China, have lengthy and complex approval processes for devs. Including strict rules about video game content, including no excessive violence or depictions of blood.

If you want to distribute there, you’ll need high-quality localization that helps you stay compliant.

## How to get started with video game localization: step-by-step

If you’re tackling localization for the first time or looking to revamp your [enterprise strategy](https://xtm.cloud/blog/enterprise-localization/), the key is building a repeatable process that can scale.

There will always be weird edge cases and technical hurdles, but a little planning prevents devs from crying into their keyboards.

Here’s a step-by-step guide to building a game [localization workflow](https://xtm.cloud/blog/localization-workflows/) that actually works.

### 1\. Bake localization into your game from the start

Whether shipping a cozy pixel RPG or the next open-world epic, you must integrate localization into your project at the outset.

Thinking ahead saves your team a mountain of technical debt and gets your game multilingual-ready.

Many popular engines already include localization kits and functions. For example, Unity has a ‘Localization Package’ and Unreal Engine has a ‘Localization Dashboard.’

Here’s what the Translation Picker in Unreal looks like:

![Image1 1](https://xtm.ai/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/image1-1-3.webp)

Source: [Epic Games](https://dev.epicgames.com/documentation/en-us/unreal-engine/localization-tools-in-unreal-engine)

These tools handle a lot of the behind-the-scenes work, like language switching and asset management. So you don’t have to figure it all out yourself.

Here are three more ways to integrate localization into your project:

- **Externalize all in-game text.** Instead of hardcoding strings, store them in resource files like JSON, XML, or CSV. That way, your game can dynamically pull in the correct language, and translators won’t have to wade through source files looking for stray bits of dialogue.
- **Plan for string length differences.** A short English word like ‘Save’ could turn into a much longer phrase in German or Spanish. Design a [flexible UI](https://xtm.cloud/blog/workflow-flexibility-tms-tools/) that scales or reflows text as needed. Otherwise, you’ll end up with buttons that look like someone jammed a novel into them.
- **Add fallback logic.** If a video game translation is missing, your game should default gracefully to English or your base language. Not throw a null reference error or spit out \[MISSING STRING\].

When you build localization into your infrastructure from the get-go, expanding for different languages later will feel like flipping a switch. Not rewriting your whole codebase.

### 3\. Give human translators as much context as possible

If you use game localization services, help in-house and freelance translators do their best work by giving them clear context in the back-end.

Even the [best linguists](https://xtm.cloud/blog/how-xtm-cloud-enables-linguists-to-work-more-efficiently-by-leveraging-the-power-of-ai/) can’t read minds. If they get a string like ‘Drop,’ they’re left guessing.

Is the character dropping loot? Dropping a friend from their party? Dropping out of school?

Help your translators help you. Give them the tools and context to turn your original script into something that feels native.

Here are three tips to save extra back-and-forth:

- **Use informative string IDs.** Ditch vague labels like ‘btn\_1’ or ‘text\_5.’ Use clear, descriptive IDs like ‘inventory.dropItem’ or ‘menu.quitGame’ so translators instantly understand the context and avoid mix-ups later.
- **Add comments and metadata.** Most translation platforms (like XTM) let you attach notes to strings. Use them to explain what the text is, where it shows up, and any character limits or placeholder rules. Bonus points for attaching screenshots or short video clips for extra clarity.
- **Share cultural reference materials.** Give translators everything they need to do a great job, including a style guide, a glossary of key terms, and sample gameplay footage. The more they understand the world, the better localization will feel in every language.

And here’s a pro suggestion that goes a long way: ask localization companies for translator names and credit them in-game.

It boosts morale and lets them proudly include your game in their portfolio.

A translator who feels invested is way more likely to go the extra mile.

### 4\. Test thoroughly before launch

Testing your localized game means checking every language version to ensure it looks good and makes sense. Culturally, yes, but also technically and visually.

Even a perfect translation can fall apart if text spills out of buttons or a joke completely misfires in another culture.

Bad localization damages trust with global audiences and leads to public callouts. Or worse, refunds.

Localization quality assurance (LQA) must include three key stages to catch issues before players ever see them:

| **Type of localization quality assurance** | **What to do** |
| --- | --- |
| Technical LQA | Run automated checks with features like [Language Guard](https://xtm.cloud/blog/introducing-language-guard/) (part of the XTM [localization tool](https://xtm.cloud/blog/localization-tools/)) to flag missing translations, broken tags, and corrupted characters (the word kind, not your favorite villains). |
| Native speaker LQA | Bring in native speakers to verify tone and cultural fit. They’ll proofread to catch awkward phrasing and context mismatches that automated tools never will. |
| In-game localization testing | Actually play the game in each language. This is where you catch the UI breaking due to long words or font rendering and spacing issues. |

Once you’ve nailed down what to test, the next step is making it easier to do consistently.

Here’s how to make testing smoother on the dev side:

- **Add a language switcher into dev builds.** This lets developers and QA teams toggle between languages instantly during testing, making it easier to debug and compare side by side.
- **Automate layout testing.** Set up automated UI tests to check translated content doesn’t break layouts — especially critical for languages with longer words or non-Latin scripts like Greek and Arabic.
- **Create a dedicated test environment.** Isolate localization testing in a stable sandbox. It keeps your main dev pipeline clean and gives testers a place to focus without disruption.

LQA is the final checkpoint before global players step into your world.

Give it the same dedication you give to gameplay QA by turning it into a thorough, repeatable process.

## 4 biggest mistakes in gaming localization and how to fix them

Even the biggest studios have blown it on localization.

For example, *Zero Wing* is a now-infamous Japanese game whose English port gave us the line, “All your base are belong to us.”

![Image5 1](https://xtm.ai/hubfs/Imported_Blog_Media/image5-1-1.webp)

Source: [TheGamer](https://www.thegamer.com/a-winner-is-you-15-of-the-worst-video-game-translation-fails/)

This mistranslation was so bad, it became a meme and a textbook example of poor localization.

Most fails are down to bad systems.

Teams rush in without planning for scalability, skip technical prep, or just assume Google Translate can handle the nuance of medieval slang. Spoiler: it can’t.

These missteps create technical debt that gets harder and more expensive to fix the deeper you get into production.

Even a game element that unintentionally offends can spark backlash. According to Global Localization Manager at King, Miguel Sepulveda:

> “Nowadays, anything can go viral very fast, and some people will not believe that it was totally unintentional. Some people will say sure, I don’t believe you, you are promoting values because that game is about battling and fighting and shooting.”

Here are four big no-nos in your video game localization process and what to do instead:

| **Gaming localization mistake** | **Solution** |
| --- | --- |
| Hardcoding text files | Store text in external files or content management systems and use string IDs. It makes swapping languages clean and fast. |
| Adding languages without market research | Do your homework. Localize for regions with actual demand. Don’t waste time translating into a language no one playing your genre uses. |
| Not putting enough time into testing | Run thorough localization QA, including native speaker reviews and technical testing. Broken UI or mistranslations kill immersion. |
| Relying solely on machine translation | Use AI to speed things up, but always involve professional translators for nuance and cultural sensitivity. |

Doing localization right from the start avoids major headaches later and keeps your studio from becoming the next meme.
